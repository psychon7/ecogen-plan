# Ecogen Product Vision

## Purpose

This document defines the long-term product vision for Ecogen based on the current platform plan, user personas, design principles, and Kimi-aligned LEED v5 research.

It is intentionally different from a roadmap or feature list. The goal is to describe the future state Ecogen exists to enable, the user world that should exist if we succeed, and the product choices that vision should force today.

---

## Vision

In the future, green building certification work will no longer feel like a manual evidence hunt spread across spreadsheets, PDFs, inboxes, and specialist silos. Sustainability teams will operate from a shared, trusted system where every compliance claim is traceable, every review decision is visible, and every project stakeholder can see real readiness without guesswork. Consultants will spend their time on judgment, coordination, and strategy rather than document assembly, while owners and project teams move forward with calm confidence because certification progress is transparent, auditable, and review-ready at every step.

## The Future We Want To Create

If Ecogen succeeds over the next 5 to 10 years:

- LEED documentation becomes a continuous operational workflow instead of a last-minute submission scramble.
- Evidence, calculations, narratives, and reviewer decisions stay connected from source to approval.
- Consultants review and guide the work; they no longer waste expert time on formatting, chasing inputs, and rebuilding the same artifacts from scratch.
- Project managers and owners see trustworthy status based on evidence readiness, review state, and risk, not optimistic point totals.
- Specialist reviewers spend time only where human expertise matters, with the exact sources, assumptions, and exceptions in front of them.
- Regional data gaps, missing APIs, and manual preparation paths are handled explicitly instead of being buried until late-stage review.

## Emotional Outcome

The feeling Ecogen should create is responsible confidence.

Users should feel:

- "I know what is ready, what is risky, and why."
- "I can trust this package enough to review it seriously."
- "I am spending my expertise where it matters."
- "Nothing important is hidden from me."

## The Potent Problem

Today, LEED teams are forced to do high-stakes compliance work inside fragmented tools and opaque workflows. They carry responsibility for certification outcomes, but the systems around them do not preserve traceability, coordinate reviewers well, or distinguish draft progress from true submission readiness. That gap creates rework, delay, avoidable risk, and unnecessary expert labor.

This is not just a productivity issue. It is a trust and workflow integrity problem in a domain where evidence quality matters.

## What Ecogen Is Really Building

Ecogen is not fundamentally trying to become "an AI that writes LEED submissions."

The deeper ambition is to become the operating layer for trustworthy sustainability compliance work:

- where evidence is structured,
- where review is built into the workflow,
- where automation is transparent,
- and where every important claim can survive scrutiny.

The initial form factor can look like a LEED evidence workspace because that is the familiar utility users need now. The long-term vision is bigger: certification and sustainability documentation should behave more like a governed system of record than a collection of disconnected deliverables.

## What This Vision Is Not

To keep the vision sharp, it explicitly rejects a few tempting but weak stories:

- Not "fully automated LEED certification"
- Not "replace LEED consultants"
- Not "generic AI drafting for sustainability teams"
- Not "support every credit equally from day one"
- Not "status dashboards without evidence integrity underneath them"

Those stories are either not believable, not defensible, or not aligned with the trust model demanded by this category.

## Strategic Choices This Vision Should Force

Because of this vision, Ecogen should consistently choose:

1. Evidence integrity over faster but opaque automation.
2. Workflow and review systems over chatbot-style interactions.
3. A narrow, high-confidence wedge over broad but unreliable coverage.
4. Submission readiness and auditability over draft volume.
5. Human approval as a design principle, not a fallback.
6. Explicit manual-preparation paths where automation is weak or unsafe.

## Near-Term Product Wedge That Fits The Vision

The best near-term expression of this vision is not "all LEED credits."

It is a trusted evidence workflow for deterministic, high-value suites where the source chain, calculations, review process, and exported package can be made meaningfully better than the current manual process. The current docs point clearly to Water Efficiency as the first commercial wedge, followed by additional suites where repeatability and evidence quality are strong enough to earn user trust.

That means the near-term story is:

"Help LEED teams prepare reviewer-ready evidence packs dramatically faster, with full traceability and human approval."

That story is strong because it is useful now and it quietly contains the larger vision.

## Product Vision Statement

Ecogen exists to create a future where sustainability certification work is transparent, traceable, and continuously review-ready, so expert teams can focus on judgment instead of paperwork and make progress with confidence rather than uncertainty.

## Vision Test

This vision is working if it helps us make hard choices. A proposed feature should strengthen at least one of these outcomes:

- Fewer hours spent assembling evidence manually
- Higher confidence in source-grounded outputs
- Better visibility into readiness, blockers, and review risk
- Clearer distinction between draft work and true submission readiness
- More reusable, standardized workflows across projects and firms

If a feature increases surface area without improving trust, traceability, readiness, or expert efficiency, it likely does not serve the vision.

## Suggested Visual Artifacts

To make the vision concrete, the next artifacts should be:

1. A future-state journey map from source ingestion to awarded credit, showing where human judgment stays in the loop.
2. A single "evidence pack" prototype that demonstrates traceability, confidence, review, and audit history in one place.
3. A project status view that distinguishes pursued, in review, submission-ready, submitted, and awarded without overstating certainty.
4. A wedge narrative for Water Efficiency that shows how the first suite expresses the long-term vision.
